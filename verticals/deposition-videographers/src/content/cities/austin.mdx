## How to Choose a Deposition Videographer in Austin

Not all videographers are created equal, and Austin's market has enough variety to make selection tricky. Here's what actually matters:

- **CLVS certification matters here.** The Certified Legal Video Specialist credential isn't just a résumé line — it means the videographer understands chain-of-custody protocols, proper swearing-in procedures, and the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure requirements for video depositions. In Travis County's increasingly complex litigation docket, courts expect professionalism.

- **Ask about their Austin coverage radius.** Depositions happen at law offices downtown on Congress Avenue, at corporate campuses out in the Domain, and at medical facilities scattered across Round Rock and Cedar Park. Confirm the videographer covers the full metro without surprise travel surcharges.

- **Remote streaming is non-negotiable in 2026.** With so many Austin-based companies having counsel in San Francisco, New York, or Houston, your videographer needs rock-solid Zoom or proprietary streaming integration. Ask about their backup internet solution — Austin's storm season can knock out connections at the worst possible moment.

- **Check their sync and encoding workflow.** The video needs to sync perfectly with the court reporter's transcript timestamps. Sloppy timecoding creates expensive problems during trial prep. Ask specifically how they handle multi-camera setups and what format they deliver in.

- **Turnaround time for the Austin market.** If you're litigating in the Western District of Texas — especially Judge Albright's famously fast patent docket in Waco — you may need expedited delivery. Confirm rush turnaround options and pricing before you book.

> **Pro Tip:** Austin's tech-heavy litigation market means many depositions involve screen-sharing exhibits and software demonstrations. Ask your videographer if they can capture picture-in-picture feeds showing both the witness and on-screen exhibits simultaneously. Not every operator has this capability, and it's a game-changer for IP and software contract disputes.

## What to Expect

Deposition videography in Austin typically runs **$500–$1,500 per session** for a standard full-day recording. Half-day rates are available and usually fall in the $400–$800 range. Expect additional charges for travel outside the central Austin metro (especially if you're deposing a witness in San Marcos or Georgetown), real-time streaming setup ($150–$300), expedited delivery ($200–$500), and extra media copies.

The process is straightforward: the videographer arrives 30–45 minutes early to set up cameras, microphones, and lighting. They'll go on the record with the standard opening statement — date, time, case caption, and identification of all parties present. From there, they manage the video record while the court reporter handles the written transcript. At breaks, they'll note the time off and on the record. At the end, they'll read the closing statement and provide you with the raw media, typically on a secure drive or encrypted upload.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest option is almost never the best option. A $350 videographer who delivers footage with audio sync issues or poor lighting will cost you far more in re-deposition fees and court headaches than a $900 professional who gets it right the first time. Travis County judges have limited patience for technical excuses.
