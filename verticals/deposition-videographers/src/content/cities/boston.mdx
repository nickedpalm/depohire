## How to Choose a Deposition Videographer in Boston

Boston's legal culture is demanding, detail-oriented, and unforgiving of sloppy work. Here's what to prioritize:

- **CLVS Certification** — In a city where attorneys from Ropes & Gray and Goodwin Procter are watching your deposition footage, the Certified Legal Video Specialist credential is non-negotiable. It signals your videographer meets federal standards and understands deposition protocol.
- **Technical and scientific deposition experience** — Boston generates an outsized volume of patent litigation, biotech disputes, and medical malpractice cases. These depositions involve complex exhibits and expert witnesses presenting detailed visual materials. Your videographer needs to handle on-camera exhibit display — diagrams, medical imaging, patent schematics — with clarity and precision.
- **Navigation of Boston logistics** — Parking near the Moakley Federal Courthouse is a nightmare. The Financial District's older buildings have unreliable elevator access for equipment. A videographer who knows Boston's physical landscape will show up on time and avoid the logistical traps that catch out-of-town providers.
- **Remote streaming excellence** — Attorneys from New York, San Francisco, and London regularly need to participate in Boston depositions remotely. Your videographer's streaming setup should be enterprise-grade — low latency, encrypted, and reliable enough to handle a full day without dropouts.
- **Coverage across eastern Massachusetts** — Depositions happen in Worcester, Springfield, Plymouth, and throughout the state. Clarify travel policies for locations outside the 128/I-95 belt and confirm your videographer handles Greater Boston's unpredictable commute times.

> **Pro Tip:** The Moakley Federal Courthouse and Suffolk County Superior Court both have AV coordinators who can advise on preferred video formats for trial presentation. If your deposition video will be played in either court, have your videographer contact the AV coordinator during post-production to confirm compatibility — it's a call that takes five minutes and can save you a day of reformatting.

## What to Expect

Deposition videography in Boston runs **$600–$1,800 per session**, reflecting the city's high cost of doing business and the premium quality expected by its legal community. Here's how the costs typically break down:

- Full-day recording (up to 7 hours of testimony)
- Half-day rates for depositions under 4 hours
- Streaming and remote participant coordination ($150–$350)
- Parking and travel within Greater Boston ($25–$75 — yes, parking is its own line item here)
- Travel to Worcester, Springfield, or Cape Cod ($100–$250)
- Expedited delivery within 24 hours (40–75% premium)

The deposition process is standard: early arrival, professional setup with calibrated audio and lighting, coordination with the court reporter, formal recording with on-camera oath administration, and delivery of time-stamped video synchronized to the written transcript. Boston videographers typically deliver in MPEG-4 with options for cloud-based access and physical media.

> **Reality Check:** Boston attorneys have seen everything, and they notice production quality. Inconsistent audio levels, poor lighting on the witness's face, or a recording that cuts out during a key admission will absolutely come up at trial — and not in a way that helps your case. This is not the market to bargain-hunt in.
