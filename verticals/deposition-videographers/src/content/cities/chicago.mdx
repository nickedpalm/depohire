## How to Choose a Deposition Videographer in Chicago

The Chicago legal market has specific demands. Here's what matters when you're hiring:

- **CLVS Certification** — The Certified Legal Video Specialist credential from NCRA is the clearest signal that a videographer understands chain-of-custody requirements, proper swearing-in procedures, and courtroom admissibility standards
- **Experience with complex litigation** — Chicago handles high-stakes commercial disputes, mass torts, and patent cases through the Northern District. Your videographer needs to be comfortable with multi-day, multi-witness depositions
- **Weather-proof reliability** — This isn't a joke. Chicago winters shut down roads and flights. Confirm your videographer has backup plans and local coverage so a January snowstorm doesn't blow your deposition schedule
- **Real-time streaming setup** — With so many national firms operating out of Chicago, remote participation is standard. Ask about platform compatibility (Zoom, Teams, proprietary systems) and encryption
- **Video-to-transcript synchronization** — Trial teams at Chicago's major firms expect synchronized video and text. Confirm the deliverable format works with your presentation software

> **Pro Tip:** If your deposition is at one of the Loop's major court reporting suites, ask whether the videographer has worked at that specific location before. Room layouts, lighting conditions, and AV hookups vary wildly between buildings, and a videographer who knows the space will set up faster and deliver better results.

## What to Expect

Deposition videography in Chicago typically runs **$500–2,000+ per session**. Most providers charge a half-day or full-day rate, with add-ons for real-time streaming, expedited turnaround, and additional media copies. Half-day rates are standard for depositions under four hours. Expect a rough-cut video within 5–7 business days, with rush delivery available at a premium.

> **Reality Check:** Don't assume the cheapest quote is saving you money. Budget videographers often cut corners on audio quality and lighting — two things that make or break a deposition video's effectiveness at trial. A $200 savings on the session means nothing if the jury can't hear the witness.
