# Writing Voice — Style Guide

A universal voice template you can apply to any niche. The tone is **smart friend who's done the homework and has no patience for BS** — equal parts rigorous and conversational, with a Milk Road-style knack for making dense material feel like a text from someone you trust.

---

## The Core Identity

You write like someone who got burned by bad advice, went down the research rabbit hole, and came back with receipts. Every piece starts from personal experience, moves through honest skepticism, and lands on specific, actionable conclusions backed by data. You're not a detached expert — you're a fellow practitioner who happens to obsess over the details.

**The vibe:** "I wasted two years and a lot of money so you don't have to."

---

## 1. Always Open With a Specific Personal Story

Never start with a definition or a generic statement. Start with a moment — vivid, specific, a little self-deprecating. The reader should think "oh, same."

**Do this:**
> I showed up to my first deposition shoot with a $400 camcorder and zero understanding of what "going on the record" actually meant. The attorney had to stop me twice because my framing cut off the witness's hands — turns out, hand gestures matter in testimony.

**Not this:**
> Deposition videography is a growing field with many opportunities.

The personal story should:
- Be specific (name the year, the gear, the moment)
- Show you were wrong or naive about something
- Create a natural bridge into "so I dug into what actually matters"

---

## 2. Position Against the Industry / Conventional Wisdom Early

Within the first few paragraphs, make it clear that the mainstream narrative is either misleading or incomplete. You're not a conspiracy theorist — you're a frustrated professional who's seen behind the curtain.

**Example framings:**
- "The gear industry wants you to think a better camera fixes bad technique."
- "Most guides on this topic read like they were written by someone who's never actually done it."
- "Popularity isn't the same as quality."

**The pattern:** [Industry/conventional wisdom does X] → [Here's why that's wrong or incomplete] → [Here's what I found when I actually looked]

---

## 3. The "Short Version" Block

Every piece gets a blockquote summary near the top. Respect the reader's time — give them the answer upfront, then earn their attention for the deep dive.

**Format:**
> **The Short Version:** [One-line answer for the most common case]. [Second line for a specific sub-case or caveat]. Below, I break down all [N] with [the specific deliverable — specs, protocols, comparisons, etc.].

---

## 4. Data-Heavy but Human

Cite specific numbers, specs, comparisons, case studies, or real-world results — but wrap them in plain English so the reader actually absorbs them. The data isn't decoration. It's the argument.

**The pattern:** [Specific detail or number] → [Plain-English translation of what it means in practice] → [Why you should care]

---

## 5. Recurring Callout Blocks

Use three signature callout types as structural rhythm:

**Reality Check** — Tempers expectations. Pushes back on hype, including your own.

**Pro Tip** — Actionable shortcut for the impatient reader.

**Important** — Warnings, compliance issues, edge cases.

---

## 6. Punchy Sentences as Emphasis

After a longer, detail-dense paragraph, drop a short declarative sentence:

- "That distinction matters more than you think."
- "Here's what actually holds up in the field."
- "There's no shortcut around it."
- "Nobody tells you this until it's too late."
- "I learned that one the expensive way."

---

## 7. Anti-Hype Framing

Never oversell. When something works, say exactly how much it works — and distinguish between "game-changing" and "noticeable improvement." The credibility comes from the restraint.

---

## 8. Comparison Tables for Scanning

When comparing multiple options, always include a quick-scan table near the top. Columns: the thing, what it's best for, price range or effort level, key trade-off.

---

## 9. Sentence-Level Patterns

**Use these:**
- "I'll be honest —"
- "Here's what most people miss:"
- "The [X] is where reality hits hardest."
- "If I could only recommend one [X], this is it."
- "Let me save you from the same traps I fell into."

**Never use these:**
- "In this article, we will explore..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "According to experts..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Let's dive in!"

---

## 10. Structure Template

1. **Personal cold open** (2-3 paragraphs) — the story, what went wrong, what sent you digging
2. **Industry/conventional wisdom callout** — why the standard advice is wrong or incomplete
3. **The Short Version** — blockquote with the bottom line and top picks
4. **"What this actually is"** — clear explanation in plain English, one vivid metaphor
5. **Comparison table** — scannable overview of options
6. **The deep dive** — each item gets specific data, personal experience, practical protocol, caveats
7. **Reality Check / Pro Tip callouts** — sprinkled throughout, max one per section
8. **Practical bottom line** — what to actually do, in what order, starting today

---

## 11. StoryBrand Framework Integration

Every page and piece of content follows the StoryBrand narrative arc — the reader is the hero, the directory is the guide.

**The framework (applied to directories):**
1. **A Character** (the reader — an attorney, project manager, business owner) **has a Problem** (needs to find a qualified professional, fast, without getting burned)
2. **And meets a Guide** (this directory — we've done the research, verified the providers, aggregated the reviews)
3. **Who gives them a Plan** (compare providers, check credentials, read real reviews, request quotes)
4. **And calls them to Action** (browse the listings, use the search, request a quote)
5. **That helps them Avoid Failure** (hiring the wrong person, wasting money, missing deadlines, getting burned by unqualified providers)
6. **And ends in Success** (finding the right professional, getting fair pricing, having confidence in their choice)

**Apply this to:**
- **Homepage:** Hero section frames the problem ("Finding a qualified [keyword] shouldn't require cold-calling 20 firms"), positions the directory as the guide ("We've verified [N] providers across [N] cities"), gives a clear plan ("Search, compare, connect"), and drives action (search bar, city grid)
- **City pages:** Frame around the local problem ("Hiring a [keyword] in [City] without local knowledge is risky"), present listings as the solution
- **Blog articles:** Open with the hero's struggle, position our research as the guide's expertise, deliver actionable plans
- **Listing pages:** The CTA ("Request a Quote") is the action step; reviews/ratings help avoid failure; the listing detail enables success

**Key phrases that reinforce the StoryBrand frame:**
- "We did the research so you don't have to."
- "Compare verified providers in your area."
- "Stop guessing. Start comparing."
- "The directory that actually gets maintained."
- "Every listing is verified. Every review is real."

---

## 12. Voice Checklist

- [ ] Does the opening paragraph make you feel like a real person is talking to you?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific personal experience?
- [ ] Does every claim have a specific number, spec, example, or data point?
- [ ] Is there a "Short Version" blockquote near the top?
- [ ] Are there at least 2 "Reality Check" or "Pro Tip" callouts?
- [ ] Does it call out industry/conventional wisdom BS without sounding paranoid?
- [ ] Would you actually say this sentence out loud to a friend?
- [ ] Is the anti-hype framing intact — honest about limitations, specific about upside?
- [ ] Are the punchy one-liners landing after dense paragraphs?
- [ ] Does the comparison table exist and is it actually useful?
